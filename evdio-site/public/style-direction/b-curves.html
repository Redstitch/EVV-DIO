Faith & Sacraments

A grace-filled life, marked in seven signs.

The sacraments are the ordinary ways Christ continues to act in the world — outward signs of an inward grace, traced from font to altar to the final anointing.

Every Catholic life moves through these seven doors. Some open once — baptism, confirmation, ordination, marriage. Others we return to again and again.

The Catechism calls the sacraments “efficacious signs of grace, instituted by Christ and entrusted to the Church.” They are not symbols of something absent but encounters with Someone present: the risen Lord, acting in water and oil, bread and wine, word and touch.

In the Diocese of Evansville, every parish is a place where this grace is offered freely — at the font where new Christians are born, at the altar where the Paschal Mystery is renewed, in the confessional where mercy is spoken aloud, at the bedside where the sick are anointed with holy oil.

Each of the seven sacraments has its own shape, its own history, its own rite. But all seven speak in the same grammar: God gives, and we receive. Taken together, they form the arc of a grace-filled life.

What follows is a short guide — the seven doors and where each of them opens. If you have questions, any priest or parish staff member in the diocese is ready to answer them.

Questions about becoming Catholic?

Every parish has a priest or staff member ready to answer. There is no form you need to fill out first, and no cost.

What is OCIA / RCIA?

The Order of Christian Initiation of Adults is the Church's process for welcoming new Catholics. It meets weekly at most parishes and culminates in reception of the sacraments at the Easter Vigil each spring.

I was baptized in another Christian tradition. Do I need to be re-baptized?

No. The Catholic Church recognizes valid baptism from most Christian traditions. Speak with your local priest about what the full reception into the Catholic Church looks like for you.

How do I know which parish I belong to?

In general, you belong to the parish whose boundaries include your home. But you are welcome to register at any parish whose community and Mass schedule suit your family best.

How do I request the Anointing of the Sick?

Call your parish office as soon as serious illness, surgery, or decline is known. Any Catholic priest can anoint; you do not need to be actively dying.

How soon should we begin marriage preparation?

The diocese asks engaged couples to meet with a priest or deacon at least six months before the wedding. Longer is welcome — preparation is meant to serve the marriage, not just the wedding.
